On the Nature of Boundaries


Individual Project - New York, 2018

Course: Major Studio I

We are bound by our body, relationships, culture, language, race, society, and country, amongst other concepts. How do these boundaries shift and evolve over time? What is our relationship with the interface of a boundary and what it contains or excludes? What would happen if these invisible boundaries were to touch? And how does human intervention shift or disrupt boundaries?


This project is a series of experiments conducted to explore the ontology of boundaries, what they represent, and how they shift and evolve over time. Each experiment aims to capture an abstract version of the dynamic system of boundaries from a different angle.

We have all these invisible boundaries in our lives that have power, but they don’t necessarily have a physical form. So, what makes up a boundary? Is it just a line on our maps and walls in our minds, or is it more fluid than that? Does it have gateways to let someone or something in or out? Or does it shift and mold to occupy different spaces over time? Does its materiality define what it can or cannot bound?

Experiment I - 'Shifting'

Caption:

Aristotle suggests that there is a ‘sharp demarcation between the inside and the outside of a thing.’ However, objects or events, and the extensions of these concepts, may have boundaries that are somewhat uncertain. From clouds and mountains, to personal and societal boundaries, all appear to elude the perception of a rigidly bounded entity. Similarly, the temporal boundaries of many events, let alone their spatial boundaries, seem to be indeterminate, always shifting.


Physical description:

A network of strings is attached to multiple nodes. The nodes shift and rotate slightly in a regular manner to loosen or tighten the strings slightly. A proximity sensor responds to human presence by making the nodes shift and rotate faster, oscillating between taught to very loose strings repeatedly.




Experiment II - 'Blurring'

Caption:

What is the material of the boundaries that bind us? Is it fluid or is it contained? Do boundaries have one dimension fewer than the entities they bound? Or can a line also be a space? Its spatially rigid, yet temporally fluid. Over time, it blurs the constructs it had been upholding. How is such blurriness to be interpreted? “An area that has not a sharp boundary-line all around, but in places just vaguely fade away into the background” (Frege). What is figure and what is ground? How does the boundary blur itself with what it bounds?


Physical description:

This experiment consists of two suspended rings made of wax. There are certain points where there is a wick reaching the wax. The wick is lit, causing the rings to slowly melt over time, and finally when all the wax has melted to the base, there is nothing left suspended.




Experiment III - 'In Transit'

Caption:

“In what way does this self-fulfilling geometrical fantasy of drawing lines in spaces contribute to the Self and Us in daily life?” Despite its discreteness, the boundary you see may not be the boundary I see. “A body’s being in motion amounts to the fact that the vector sum of the motions of zillions of restless particles, averaged over time, is non-zero, hence it makes no sense to speak of the instant at which a body stops moving”, (Galton 1994). Yet if this moment is prolonged and your attention is present, the pause comes into existence.


Physical description:

There are two transparent tubes connected to axles that are rotated 180 degrees back and forth by servo motors. There is a motion sensor in the front, which responds by making the tubes oscillate faster when motion is detected. The tubes contain white powder and have a series of holes in a straight line, which let powder flow out for a moment when the holes are parallel to the base, before the tubes rotate back up.

Conclusion:

Most boundaries are our own constructs, yet they can acquire a life of their own, influencing the behavior of objects and people in their vicinity and vice versa. The materiality of boundaries defines how much power and agency a boundary has. Usually, the material is not tangible, instead it’s made up of concepts. These intangible characters that exist with and around boundaries are abstracted in tangible materials in these three experiments, to represent larger concepts that impact us. The changes in the three respective forms over time, allows us to observe the ever-changing nature of boundaries and asks questions about who or what intervenes, disrupts or affects these boundaries.
